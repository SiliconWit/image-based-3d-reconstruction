Photogrammetry is a discipline that has heavily been influenced by the progression in computer science technology. It is a discipline has experienced significant shifts in its evolution, especially following the shift from analogue to analytical and digital methods since its conception as early the 19th century[[WaybackMachine2017]]. Even though its inception was based on topographical mapping, photogrammetry today is considered a discipline on its own, distinct from remote sensing. The main distinction between photogrammetry and remote sensing lies in their applications. Photogrammetry focuses on creating maps and determining precise three-dimensional positions of points, whereas remote sensing involves analysing and interpreting images to gather information about the Earth's land and water areas[[schenkIntroductionPhotogrammetry]]. As a scientific field of study, a lot of work has been done in development of algorithms that execute the stitching of images to come up with a three-dimensional model. 

In a standard photogrammetric process, the pipeline initiates with the orientation of images, depending on a wealth of feature matches between the images and the triangulation of a sparse point cloud, commonly referred to as Structure from Motion (SfM). This involves using either incremental or global bundle adjustment (BA) techniques. Following this, a dense 3D reconstruction is carried out, typically known as Multiple View Stereo (MVS). This step aims to enhance the sparse point cloud by determining the depth value for nearly every pixel correspondence in three-dimensional space[[OpensourceImagebased3D]]. These pipelines have been packaged into software programs, which include, in addition to all the algorithms needed, a user interface for convenient use of the pipeline. Both proprietary and open source alternatives exist in the market. Among the opens source options are, Meshroom(AliceVision)[[ griwodzAliceVisionMeshroomOpensource2021]], ColMap[[schonbergerStructurefromMotionRevisited2016]], OpenMVG + OpenMVS[[ moulonOpenMVGOpenMultiple2017,cdcOpenMVSOpenMultiView2023]].

## Applications
Photogrammetry has gone on to be used as a tool across various disciplines. In [[syngelakiNewMethodNoninvasive2018]] a new non-destructive technique for estimating biomass using stereo photogrammetry was created, involving a digital single-lens reflex camera (DSLR), an illuminated platform, and an Arduino board for multi-angle image capture. Specialized software for stereo photogrammetry was developed to reconstruct three-dimensional models of the samples. This method was effectively tested on plant tissue cultures to calculate their specific growth rates, showing significant promise for applications in biological sciences. In [[lauriaDetailedMethodCreating2022]], a method is described for producing high-resolution 3D digital models of human crania using close-range photogrammetry, specifically employing Structure-from-Motion (SfM) techniques. This approach accurately captures chromatic details and shape with a minimal scale error, offering a simple and cost-effective solution for realistic model creation. The paper emphasizes the technique's suitability for various applications in virtual anthropology, osteological studies, and archaeology, highlighting its benefits in preservation and restoration efforts. 
In [[liuComparisonOpensourceThreedimensional2023]] photogrammetry is applied to create 3D models of maize roots, demonstrating its effectiveness in agricultural research. They use open-source 3D reconstruction pipelines like COLMAP and VisualSFM, which offer a cost-effective, high-throughput alternative to more expensive imaging methods for capturing root architecture. The research involves two computational experiments assessing the quality of 3D models and the accuracy of trait measurements, showing that the COLMAP pipeline efficiently reduces the number of images needed without compromising model accuracy. This approach successfully applies photogrammetry to complex root structure analysis, providing valuable insights for fields such as virtual agriculture and plant phenotyping. In [[guerraFlightGogglesPhotorealisticSensor2019]], Guerra et al. introduce FlightGoggles, a simulator utilizing photogrammetry for photorealistic sensor simulation in robotic vehicles. This technology efficiently creates realistic 3D models from photographs, significantly enhancing robotic applications and simulations. FlightGoggles enables realistic human-vehicle interactions in a virtual environment, demonstrating its practical use in scenarios such as autonomous drone racing and visual inertial navigation. The simulator's innovative use of photogrammetry marks a significant advancement in robotics testing and development, showcasing its importance in high-speed flight simulation and safe, interactive testing environments​. It is evident that photogrammetry, despite its ubiquitous nature, has a lot to offer in various fields and in impactful ways this is mainly thanks to its ability to capture textures.
## Information path planning
Information Path Planning (IPP) is a strategic approach used in robotics and autonomous systems to optimize the collection of information or data from the environment. The goal of IPP is to determine the most effective route or series of actions an autonomous agent (like a robot or drone) should take to gather the required data efficiently and accurately. Next Best View(NBV) on the other hand is a component of IPP  that focuses on the immediate subsequent sensor position(s) to maximize information gain. To this end, information path planning algorithms employ a utility function that evaluates the potential usefulness or value of certain actions or paths that a robot might take given a given input with the description of the environment. These descriptions are essentially representations of the environment with respect to a metric of interest. The result of the utility function will go on to influence the planning algorithm.[[chosetPrinciplesRobotMotion2005]]. Furthermore, planning algorithms may make use of model-free and model-based techniques. In the former case, there is a reference model to the world, while in the latter case, there is no prior knowledge of the surroundings [[zhangTHREEDIMENSIONALPATHPLANNING2020]]. 

In photogrammetry, more so in close-range photogrammetry, there is relatively scarce literature on NBV compared to other reconstruction techniques used in Exploration and Navigation. This is partly due to the nature of the primary sensor in photogrammetry more time is needed to get meaningful information out of collected data in real time. Nevertheless, traditional means of automation of close-range photogrammetry capture processes have been based on generic patterns of movements of the target object its a local axis while the sensor is at a still position facing the target object [[bucchiRecommendationsImprovingPhoto2020 ]][[marshallAUTOMATINGPHOTOGRAMMETRY3D2019 ]][[porterSimplePhotogrammetryRig2016]] or the sensor revolving around the target object [[lambProgrammatic3DPrinting2018 ]][[samaanCLOSERANGEPHOTOGRAMMETRICTOOLS2013]]. In  [[marshallAUTOMATINGPHOTOGRAMMETRY3D2019]]  a system for automating photogrammetry in 3D digitization is presented, which includes planning elements for capturing images of small artifacts. A specially developed software allows for inputting object dimensions and camera specifications to automatically generate camera positions for image capture, ensuring optimal data acquisition. The study shows that automating photogrammetry balances data capture and processing variables effectively, achieving high-quality scans with resource efficiency. This system is deemed suitable for heritage institutions and small enterprises, considering their budget constraints and resource availability. In [[zhangTHREEDIMENSIONALPATHPLANNING2020]] a 3D path planning method for UAVs aimed at enhancing photogrammetric reconstruction. Utilizing a model-based approach, the method leverages a coarse model obtained from an initial flight to inform the planning process. It focuses on improving the completeness and precision of the 3D reconstruction by analysing the initial model and planning additional viewpoints with photogrammetric constraints. The proposed method demonstrated superior performance in acquiring a more complete reconstruction with similar precision, compared to an existing 3D planning method, while using fewer images. The authors conclude that this approach can be applied before image alignment to reduce redundancy, potentially optimizing large-scale image processing for 3D reconstructions. In [[peraltaNextBestViewPolicy2020]], Scan-RL, a reinforcement learning-based algorithm, to develop a Next-Best View (NBV) policy for drone-based 3D reconstruction. This approach is aimed at overcoming the limitations of manual or basic automated flight paths in capturing complete 3D models, especially in occluded areas. Scan-RL optimizes the scanning path, achieving more efficient 3D reconstructions in fewer steps and shorter distances compared to traditional methods like circular paths. The algorithm's effectiveness is demonstrated through the Houses3K dataset, consisting of 3D house models, which also facilitates further research in view planning and aerial robotics. The study shows that the learned NBV policy can be successfully applied to multiple houses, indicating its potential for generalization and real-world application. The authors highlight the algorithm's contribution to improving 3D reconstruction processes and suggest future work in deploying these policies in real drone operations​.

In summary, Information Path Planning (IPP) and its component, Next Best View (NBV), represent significant advancements in the field of robotics and photogrammetry, particularly in the optimization of data collection and 3D reconstruction. While IPP provides a strategic framework for determining effective routes for autonomous agents, NBV specifically focuses on selecting the most informative subsequent sensor positions. The integration of these methodologies has shown potential in enhancing the efficiency and accuracy of photogrammetric reconstructions, as demonstrated in various studies. For instance, the application of Scan-RL in drone-based 3D reconstruction exemplifies how learning-based algorithms can optimize path planning, outperforming traditional methods in complex environments. These advancements not only streamline the data acquisition process, but also open new avenues for research and practical application in fields requiring detailed environmental modelling and analysis. The ongoing development and application of these techniques will likely continue to revolutionize how autonomous systems interact with and understand their surroundings.
# Conceptual Framework
This literature review has highlighted various solution that aim to tackle the significant challenges in conventional photogrammetry image capture, including the labor-intensive nature of human-led processes, physical constraints, potential information gaps, inconsistencies in camera movement, and inefficiencies in coverage and time. These issues underscore the need for more sophisticated, automated methods in close-range photogrammetry, particularly in contexts with complex geometries and self-occlusions. The proposed work looks to the utilisation of depth maps generated from single images for online information path planning represents a novel and promising approach. This methodology aims to address these critical gaps by enhancing the accuracy and efficiency of the image capture process. By automating the path planning and incorporating depth information, the new approach seeks to mitigate human error, ensure comprehensive coverage, and optimize data acquisition. This innovative strategy not only promises to revolutionize the field of monocular-based close-range photogrammetry but also sets the stage for further advancements in automated photogrammetric techniques.
