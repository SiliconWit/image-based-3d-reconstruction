The following is a list of problems during  conventional  means of capture for photogrammetry,
1. **Human-Led Image Capture Challenges:** Manually capturing images for photogrammetry is labor-intensive and can introduce inconsistencies due to human error or oversight.
2. **Physical Constraints:** Humans might face challenges accessing certain viewpoints, especially those that are too low, too high, or potentially dangerous.
3. **Potential for Information Gaps:** Traditional methods might result in camera angles that omit essential details, creating gaps in the reconstructed model.
4. **Camera Movement Inconsistencies:** Manual capturing can introduce irregularities in camera movement and positioning. These inconsistencies can lead to variations in perceived scale and depth, especially when capturing complex or detailed subjects. This makes the post-processing and reconstruction phase challenging due to potential disparities in the data.
5. **Redundant Coverage and Time Efficiency:** Often, there's unnecessary redundancy with some areas being over-captured and others under-captured. This not only increases the computational workload but also extends the capture phase.

In traditional photogrammetry, the capture process is predominantly manual, leading to a plethora of challenges that compromise the efficacy and quality of reconstructions. Firstly, the human-led image capture is not only labor-intensive but also fraught with inconsistencies stemming from human errors or oversight. This is compounded by physical constraints, where human operators may find difficulty in accessing certain crucial viewpoints, be it due to their extreme height, depth, or inherent danger. Moreover, these manual methods often result in camera orientations that inadvertently omit vital details, culminating in significant information gaps in the final reconstruction. Such practices further introduce irregularities in camera movement and positioning, causing variations in the perceived scale and depth, particularly when imaging intricate subjects. This unpredictability complicates the subsequent post-processing and reconstruction phase. Furthermore, a manual approach can lead to redundant coverage, where certain areas might be excessively captured, unnecessarily prolonging the capture process and amplifying the computational demands during reconstruction. This inefficiency and these inaccuracies underscore an urgent need for a more systematic and automated solution to optimize the photogrammetric capture process.
