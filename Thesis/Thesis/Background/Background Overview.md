In the dynamic landscape of digital technology, 3D reconstruction has emerged as a transformative process that bridges the gap between physical reality and digital representation. At its core, 3D reconstruction involves capturing the intricate shape and visual characteristics of real-world objects and environments, subsequently translating this data into highly detailed three-dimensional models. Principles at the heart of 3D reconstruction, irrespective of the chosen technique, are rooted in the manipulation of visual data to ascertain spatial relationships. Whether through structured light scanning, photogrammetry, laser scanning, or other methods, the essential process involves employing a sensor to capture multiple perspectives of the subject, exploiting the subtle shifts in these viewpoints to infer depth and dimension. Algorithms then synchronize these viewpoints, constructing a holistic 3D model that encapsulates the geometry and intricate details of the subject. The convergence of computer vision, spatial mechanics and mathematics is instrumental in this endeavor, harmonizing the captured data to generate an accurate digital representation of the physical entity.

The relevance of 3D reconstruction extends far beyond the realms of academia, resonating through fields as diverse as archaeology, entertainment, medicine, and robotics. The ability to craft accurate digital twins of historical relics, living organisms, architectural wonders, and dynamic urban landscapes has become an indispensable asset. Beyond mere replication, these 3D renditions empower researchers, engineers, and creators to delve into virtual explorations, precision diagnostics, streamlined designs, and autonomous navigation. 

Photogrammetry, stands out as one of the most popular means for 3D reconstruction owing to its high accessibility and versatility while still providing up to submillimeter accuracy[[luhmannCloseRangePhotogrammetry3D2019]] in its resultant reconstructions. Etymologically photogrammetry stems from three Greek words, *phos* meaning light, *gramma* which means draw, and *metron* which means measure; all three compounded to imply the measurement from photographs.[[saifPhotogrammetryBriefHistorical2022]]. Fundamentally, photogrammetry seeks to establish geometric relationships between an image of an object and the object as it was during the imaging event. The science of photogrammetry has evolved over centuries in terms of how the image is captured, and how the aforementioned relationship is obtained. It has gone from purely analog systems utilizing opto-mechanical and electronic components, to a wholly analytical one employing mathematical models and digital capture systems.[[mikhailIntroductionModernPhotogrammetry2001a]], [[saifPhotogrammetryBriefHistorical2022]]. That said, a broader yet modern definition of photogrammetry as put by the American Society of Photogrammetry is " the art, science, and technology of obtaining reliable information about physical objects and the environment, through processes of recording, measuring, and interpreting images and patterns of electromagnetic radiant energy and other phenomena". 

Photogrammetry offers a non-contact approach to capturing the intricate dimensions of our physical world setting it apart from other reconstruction techniques especially in the context of metrology. Its non-contact essence is rooted in its astute utilization of light, which, as it traverses the space between the object and the camera, remains untethered from physical intervention. By merely capturing images from various vantage points, photogrammetry transforms the interplay of light into a digital blueprint of an object's shape and structure. This process not only respects the integrity of the subject but also avoids any potential alterations or distortions that contact-based techniques might inadvertently introduce.

This technique's prowess, however, extends far beyond geometry. Photogrammetry possesses a unique ability to capture not just the structure, but also its textures and colors. As a tapestry of images is woven into a 3D reconstruction, the intricate details, hues, and nuances of the object's surface are woven into the digital twin. This fusion of shape and appearance amplifies the realism and utility of photogrammetric models, particularly in applications demanding visual authenticity. While its contemporaries, such as structured light scanning and laser scanning, may offer indisputable prowess in precision, they often remain ensnared within their niches, necessitating specialized equipment and expertise. Photogrammetry, in stark contrast, beckons with its simplicity. Its approachability obliterates the steep learning curves and equipment costs, allowing not-so-technically-inclined personnel to partake in the digitization process.

Being a versatile discipline, photogrammetry has numerous ways in which it can be classified, This can be through The camera's position, and its distance relative to the object, the number of measurement images, method of recording and processing, availability of measurement results and  area of application. [[luhmannCloseRangePhotogrammetry3D2019]]. When considering the position and distance of the camera relative to the object, this study seeks to look into close-range photogrammetry where the distance between the target and the object ranges from 0.2-200m with accuracies from ~0.1mm to 1cm[[luhmannCloseRangePhotogrammetry3D2019]]. When considering the number of images, multiple-image photogrammetry is considered, where bundle triangulation is performed on multiple images for reconstruction purposes. The 3D reconstruction process of based on photogrammetry is more often than not the same, regardless of the classification of photogrammetry. Input images are captured first, and afterwards the images are fed into a 3D reconstruction pipeline, which largely consists of structure from motion as well as Multi-view stereo algorithms that work contiguously to give a 3D output. The 3D output may further undergo post-processing, which involves cleaning out unwanted parts from the reconstruction. There is plenty of research works undertaken on 3D reconstruction pipelines that have often led to the development and improvement of photogrammetry-based 3D reconstruction software. However, the precursor to the reconstruction computations-image capture- is just as important given that 3D reconstruction is a "Garbage-In-Garbage-Out" process.
